This article investigates a practical formation control problem of multisatellite system, in which a group of small satellites are required to reach and maintain a desired geometric configuration under communication and measurement constraints. Toward this goal, we consider each satellite uses its local coordinate system and senses its neighboring satellites to obtain angle measurements. A formation control scheme using only the angle measurements is proposed to form the desired configuration. Moreover, the triangular and polygonal configuration cases are considered, in which the sum of interior angles of polygons being a constant is utilized to guarantee the stability of the determined final configuration. I. INTRODUCTION
Satellite formation flying has been of growing interest to environmental measurement and Earth observation due to its large geographic coverage and rich sensing functionalities. In particular, the technological development of small satellites enables satellite formations to excel in their modularity, low cost, and distributed functionality [1]. In order to take advantage of satellite formations to implement space missions, several formation configurations have been extensively studied for engineering applications, such as a triangular formation for detecting gravitational waves [2] and tracking of a missile launch [3], and a hexagonal regular formation for building large antennas [4].
In satellite formation control, the position-based control approach is widely used to maintain the formation configurations. Satellites measure their own positions and velocities through the global positioning system or inertial navigation system. The desired formation can be achieved by the feedback control of the individual satellite's absolute position without communication between each other [5]. Under a properly designed communication topology and sensing topology, the displacement-based formation control, which requires that satellites obtain relative positions with respect to their neighbors, is applied to improve the robustness of formation keeping. During the last decade, the displacement-based formation control has been widely applied to satellite formation control that can be either centralized or decentralized [6], [7]. To achieve the desired formation configuration, the consensus theory is usually taken to design the formation control law [8], [9], [10].
In recent years, the bearing-based formation control, which determines the desired formation by sensing the relative bearings with respect to neighbors [11], [12], has been extensively studied for multiagent systems. For satellite formation control, the line of sight (LOS) vector acquired by vision-based sensors and star trackers can be used as the bearing measurements for control. Gurfil [13] studied the cyclic pursuit formation problem where each satellite tracks the LOS to a single neighboring satellite so as to form a cyclic formation. Since each satellite only acquires the information from one neighboring satellite in the cyclic topology, the cyclic satellite formation needs the minimal number of communication connections and measurements in which the information interaction among the satellites is effectively reduced.
However, the position-based, displacement-based, and bearing-based formation control require the measurements to be in a global coordinate frame or a common coordinate frame shared by all the agents [14], which means the

To relax this restriction, some formation control schemes based on distance or angle measurements are investigated [15], [16]. In the distance-based formation control, the agents measure relative positions of their neighbors with respect to their own local coordinate systems. To ensure the uniqueness of the desired formation, the distance rigidity, in which the constraints on those desired distances are sufficient for the maintenance of the distances of any pair of agents, is used to describe the desired configuration [17]. In particular, the angle-based formation control attracted much attention due to the fact that the angle measurements are cheaper and more accessible [14]. More importantly, angle measurements can be taken from bearings defined and measured in satellites' local coordinate systems without a common sense of orientation. Basiri [18] first presented the result on provably stable angle-only formation control for a triangular formation case. Further, Bishop [19] developed a distributed control law for triangular formation control using a mixture of distance and angle information. This mixture allows the agents to have heterogeneous sensing capabilities. Since the angle measurements are scalars and are invariant to motions including formations' translations, rotations, and scalings, a new angle rigidity scheme was developed to solve the formation control problem by using angle constraints [20]. Note that the existing angle-based control results have focused on the angle-based control for single-integrator agent models. Thus, it is necessary to investigate more realistic applications of angle-based formation control.
From the practical point of view, unique features of the small satellite formation are threefold. First, less and lower cost computational instruments are essential for the satellite formation control because small satellites are constrained by size and power consumption. Second, the previous studies on formation control requires that satellites sense measurements with respect to the global coordinate frame or have the common orientations of local coordinate frames. This demands for the precise attitude control of all satellites. Third, there is no enough infrastructure for all-to-all communication among small satellites. Moreover, technical challenges arise from communication delays and topology switching. These arguments motivate us to look for a cheaper and more reliable formation control scheme.
In this article, we consider the circular satellite formation control problem, where only angle measurements are available to each satellite. The angle measurements can be obtained by star trackers or cameras [21], [22]. Moreover, a satellite formation control scheme for the practical dynamics is proposed, in which the knowledge of the positions of neighboring satellites and communication topology are unnecessary to construct the formation control law. In particular, the orientations of the local measurement coordinate systems of all satellites are unnecessary to be aligned to each other.

REMARK 6 In fact, although the desired polygon formation is achieved, the flying radius of the formation is ambiguous when all satellites converge to the formation stability. To determine the flying radius, by using the rule of the sum of interior angles, the motion scheme for the polygon formation is that one satellite maintains the desired circular orbit without the control input, and its flying radius (r1 = p1 ) is the desired radius r. Then, the rest of satellites construct the desired polygon configuration under the polygonal formation control law. As a result, the flying radii of all satellites converge to the desired radius r at the steady state.

V. CONCLUSION
In this article, we exploit satellite formation control problem with the purpose to mitigate the position/communication requirements that are inevitable with traditional treatments in the same venue. More specifically, for the triangular formation configuration, we demonstrate that the desired configuration can be determined by any two satellites attaining the expected angles, while, for the polygonal formation configuration, the determination for the formation flying radius is intricate in the sense that one satellite's relative orbit is fixed and the others construct the desired formation shape. In addition, in order to ensure the rigidity of the eventual configuration, a simple construction strategy is proposed allowing that each satellite only senses one angle. Simulation results demonstrate that all satellites can form the desired formation configurations while maintaining their own desired motions.
