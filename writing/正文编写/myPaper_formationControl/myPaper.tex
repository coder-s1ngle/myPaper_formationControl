\begin{abstract}
This paper studies the distributed formation control problem of four agents forming a planar square in 3-D space. For a square, the concyclic nature of the vertices renders unsigned angle constraints alone insufficient to determine a unique labeled formation---two reflected configurations with opposite cyclic connection orders satisfy the same angle constraints. To address this reflection ambiguity, an undirected plane-normal control scheme is introduced. The desired formation configuration is thus described by local angle constraints, selected distance constraints, an undirected plane-normal constraint, and a coplanarity constraint. A distributed formation controller combining these five terms is formulated to achieve the desired square shape, select the intended reflection branch, maintain coplanarity, and track a desired common velocity. A minimal internal error coordinate is constructed, and local exponential stability of the nominal closed-loop system is proved. The coplanarity term is shown, via a perturbation analysis, to provide first-order sensitivity to out-of-plane deviations where angle-based terms provide only second-order sensitivity, establishing its necessity for 3-D planar formation control.
\end{abstract}

Formation control, multi-agent systems, undirected normal control, reflection ambiguity, coplanarity control, angle rigidity.

\section{Introduction}
\IEEEPARstart{M}{ulti-agent} formation control has been widely studied because a coordinated group can perform tasks that are difficult for a single vehicle, such as cooperative transportation, search and rescue, and environmental monitoring. Compared with position-based and displacement-based methods, local sensing-based formation control reduces the dependence on global positioning and common reference frames. In this direction, bearing- and angle-based methods are especially attractive since relative direction information can be obtained from onboard vision or line-of-sight sensors.

Angle-rigidity-based formation control has been developed to stabilize formations using local angle measurements \cite{chen2021angleRigidity}. Since angle constraints are invariant under translation, rotation, and scaling, additional information is generally required when the scale or orientation of a formation must be prescribed. Recent studies have therefore introduced distance, orientation, or leader information to resolve the remaining degrees of freedom \cite{chen2022stabilizing,chen2023angleConstrained,li2025localInfo}. Most existing angle-based planar formation results either assume that agents are already coplanar or operate in a 2-D setting. When agents maneuver in 3-D space, maintaining coplanarity of a nominally planar formation becomes a nontrivial control problem.

This paper considers the formation control of four agents forming a planar square in 3-D space. The desired configuration is described by a square-shape constraint, a scale constraint, a plane-normal constraint, and a coplanarity constraint. We show that unsigned angle constraints and distance constraints alone admit two reflected solution families with opposite cyclic connection orders, and that angle-based control terms provide only second-order sensitivity to out-of-plane deviations. An undirected plane-normal control scheme is introduced to address the reflection ambiguity, and an explicit coplanarity term is shown to be necessary for maintaining coplanarity. A distributed formation controller combining these terms is proposed, and local exponential stability of the nominal closed-loop system is established by constructing minimal internal error coordinates.

\section{Problem Formulation}

\subsection{Agent Dynamics}

Consider a group of four agents moving in 3-D space. The index set is
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:vertex-set}
\mathcal V=\{1,2,3,4\}.
\end{equation}
The dynamics of agent \(i\) are described by the double integrator
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:agent-dynamics}
\dot p_i = v_i,\qquad
\dot v_i = u_i,
\qquad i\in\mathcal V,
\end{equation}
where \(p_i\in\mathbb R^3\) and \(v_i\in\mathbb R^3\) denote the position and velocity of agent \(i\) in a fixed inertial frame, and \(u_i\in\mathbb R^3\) is the control input.

For any \(i,j\in\mathcal V\), define the relative position vector, inter-agent distance, and bearing vector as
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:bearing-definition}
q_{ij}=p_j-p_i,\qquad
d_{ij}=\|q_{ij}\|,\qquad
b_{ij}=\frac{q_{ij}}{\|q_{ij}\|}.
\end{equation}
The unsigned interior angle formed by agents \(j\), \(i\), and \(k\) is
\begin{equation} 
\label{eq:angle-definition}
\alpha_{jik}=\arccos\left(b_{ij}^{T}b_{ik}\right),
\qquad \alpha_{jik}\in[0,\pi].
\end{equation}
Since defining signed angles in 3-D requires additional reference directions or aligned coordinate frames, we adopt unsigned angles throughout \cite{chen2023angleRigidity3D}.

The following assumptions are made.

\emph{Assumption 1:} Each agent is equipped with LOS sensing to acquire the relative direction information required by the formation controller.

\emph{Assumption 2:} Only agent~1 measures the distances to its two neighboring agents, namely \(d_{12}\) and \(d_{14}\), and the measured distances can be transmitted to the corresponding neighboring agents when needed.

\emph{Assumption 3:} The initial formation lies in a neighborhood of the desired configuration \(\mathcal S(\ell^*,n_d)\), and the positions of the agents are distinct with nondegenerate reference triangles \(\{1,2,4\}\) and \(\{2,3,4\}\).

\emph{Remark 1:} Assumptions~1--3 specify the onboard sensing, communication, and initialization requirements. Each agent requires only a bearing sensor, except agent~1 which additionally carries a distance sensor. The communication burden is limited to two scalar distances sent from agent~1 to agents~2 and~4. No alignment of local coordinate frames is required, and no global position information is needed.

\subsection{Formation Constraints}

The in-plane square shape is encoded by the angle constraint set
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:angle-set}
\mathcal A=\{(2,1,4),(3,2,1),(4,3,2),(1,4,3)\},
\end{equation}
where each tuple \((j,i,k)\in\mathcal A\) denotes the interior angle with agent \(i\) as the vertex. The desired angles are
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:desired-angle}
\alpha_{jik}^*=\frac{\pi}{2},
\qquad (j,i,k)\in\mathcal A.
\end{equation}

Angle constraints determine the square shape but leave the formation scale free. To fix the scale, we introduce two distance-constrained edges associated with agent~1 \cite{li2025localInfo}:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:distance-edge-set}
\mathcal E_d=\{(1,2),(1,4)\}.
\end{equation}
The desired distances are
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:desired-distance}
d_{ij}=d_{ij}^*=\ell^*,
\qquad
(i,j)\in\mathcal E_d,
\end{equation}
where \(\ell^*>0\) is the desired side length of the square formation. The constraint set is illustrated in Figure~\ref{fig:square_constraints}.

\begin{figure}[!t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=3.45in,trim=6pt 6pt 6pt 6pt,clip]{figures/fig_1}
\caption{Square formation constraints. The four interior angle constraints \(\mathcal A\) determine the square shape, while the two distance-constrained edges \(\mathcal E_d\) fix the formation scale.}
\label{fig:square_constraints}
\end{figure}

Although the angle and distance constraints together specify the in-plane square shape and scale, they do not determine the orientation of the formation plane in 3-D space: the normal vector of the plane spanned by the four agents is a free degree of freedom under these constraints.

In the counter-drone capture net scenario, the net opening plane must face the incoming target; the plane orientation is therefore a prescribed control objective. To prescribe the orientation in 3-D, we introduce a plane-normal constraint. The reference plane of the formation is defined by the ordered triplet of agents~1, 2, and~4, whose unit normal vector is
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:normal-124}
n_{124}=\frac{b_{12}\times b_{14}}
{\|b_{12}\times b_{14}\|}.
\end{equation}

A natural candidate for the normal-direction error is the cross product
\(n_{124}\times n_d\),
where \(n_d\in\mathbb R^3\), \(\|n_d\|=1\), is the prescribed target plane normal.
This quantity vanishes whenever \(n_{124}\) is parallel to \(n_d\),
which occurs at both \(n_{124}=n_d\) and \(n_{124}=-n_d\).
At the latter, the error is zero despite \(n_{124}\neq n_d\);
the cross product cannot distinguish the two opposite alignment directions.

For the capture net application, the sign of the net opening normal is immaterial:
\(n_d\) and \(-n_d\) represent the same physical net opening orientation
as shown in Figure~\ref{fig:orientation_branch}.
The sign ambiguity at the target is therefore acceptable in itself.
However, because the raw error treats \(+n_d\) as the unique signed target,
the equilibrium at \(n_{124}=-n_d\) is unstable under the control law:
a formation near \(-n_d\) is driven toward \(+n_d\)
rather than settling at the closer branch.

To make both branches stable, we define the candidate set
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:normal-set}
\mathcal N_d=\{n_d,-n_d\},
\qquad \|n_d\|=1,
\end{equation}
and select the branch closest to the current normal direction:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:normal-representative}
\bar n_d=\operatorname*{arg\,max}_{\eta\in\mathcal N_d}
\left(n_{124}^{T}\eta\right).
\end{equation}
The normal-direction error is then defined with respect to the selected branch:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:normal-error}
e_n=n_{124}\times \bar n_d.
\end{equation}
When \(e_n=0\), the formation plane is aligned with the target plane in the undirected sense, i.e., \(n_{124}=\pm n_d\). With the branch selection~\eqref{eq:normal-representative}, both \(n_{124}=n_d\) and \(n_{124}=-n_d\) are stable equilibria of the closed-loop system.

In 3-D maneuvering, the four agents may drift out of the reference plane. To enforce coplanarity, we require agent~3 to lie on the reference plane \(\{1,2,4\}\). Using its local bearings, agent~3 measures the coplanarity error as
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:coplanar-error}
e_{\mathrm{cop}}=b_{31}^{T}n_{234},
\qquad
n_{234}=\frac{b_{32}\times b_{34}}{\|b_{32}\times b_{34}\|},
\end{equation}
where \(n_{234}\) is the unit normal of plane \(\{2,3,4\}\). The absolute value \(|e_{\mathrm{cop}}|\) is proportional to the volume of the tetrahedron formed by the four agents, which vanishes if and only if the agents are coplanar. Hence \(e_{\mathrm{cop}}=0\) precisely when agent~3 lies on \(\{1,2,4\}\) as shown in Figure~\ref{fig:coplanar_constraint}.

Combining the above constraints, the desired formation configuration set is
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:desired-formation-set}
\mathcal S(\ell^*,n_d)=\bigl\{p\in\mathbb R^{12}\mid
\begin{aligned}
&\alpha_{jik}=\alpha_{jik}^*,\; (j,i,k)\in\mathcal A,\\
&d_{ij}=d_{ij}^*,\; (i,j)\in\mathcal E_d,\\
&e_{\mathrm{cop}}=0,\; |n_{124}^{T}n_d|=1\bigr\}.
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
The condition \(|n_{124}^{T}n_d|=1\) requires the reference-plane normal to be parallel to \(n_d\) in an unoriented sense, i.e., the formation plane must be parallel to the target plane without prescribing a signed normal direction.

\centering
\includegraphics[width=3.45in,trim=2pt 2pt 2pt 2pt,clip]{figures/fig_2}
\caption{Two labeled square configurations satisfying the same angle and distance constraints. They correspond to opposite cyclic connection orders and are distinguished by the sign of the plane normal.}
\label{fig:orientation_branch}
\end{figure}

\begin{figure}[!t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=3.45in,trim=2pt 2pt 2pt 2pt,clip]{figures/fig_3}

\label{fig:coplanar_constraint}
\end{figure}

With the above setup, the formation control problem is stated as follows.

\emph{Problem 1:} Consider the four-agent system \eqref{eq:agent-dynamics} under Assumptions~1 and~3. Design a distributed formation controller such that the closed-loop system achieves
\begin{align}
\lim_{t\to+\infty}
\bigl(\alpha_{jik}(t)-\alpha_{jik}^*\bigr)&=0,
& (j,i,k)&\in\mathcal A, \label{eq:angle-convergence}\\
\lim_{t\to+\infty}
\bigl(d_{ij}(t)-d_{ij}^*\bigr)&=0,
& (i,j)&\in\mathcal E_d, \label{eq:distance-convergence}\\
\lim_{t\to+\infty}e_{\mathrm{cop}}(t)&=0, \label{eq:coplanar-convergence}\\
\lim_{t\to+\infty}\|e_n(t)\|&=0, \label{eq:normal-convergence}\\
\lim_{t\to+\infty}
\bigl(v_i(t)-v_c^*(t)\bigr)&=0,
& i&\in\mathcal V. \label{eq:velocity-convergence}
\end{align}
Equations \eqref{eq:angle-convergence}--\eqref{eq:coplanar-convergence} together with \eqref{eq:normal-convergence} imply convergence to \(\mathcal S(\ell^*,n_d)\), while \eqref{eq:velocity-convergence} ensures velocity tracking.

\section{Main Results}
In this section, we present the distributed formation control law, establish the necessity of the coplanarity constraint, and analyze the closed-loop stability.

\subsection{Control Law Design}

The distributed formation control laws are proposed as follows:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:total-control}
\begin{aligned}
u_1&=-k_\theta e_{\theta_1}(b_{12}+b_{14})+k_d(e_{12}b_{12}+e_{14}b_{14})\\
    &\quad +k_n(e_n\times r_1)-k_v\tilde v_1,\\
u_2&=-k_\theta e_{\theta_2}(b_{23}+b_{21})-k_d e_{12}b_{12}
      +k_n(e_n\times r_2)\\
    &\quad -k_v\tilde v_2,\\
u_3&=-k_\theta e_{\theta_3}(b_{34}+b_{32})-k_{\mathrm{cop}}e_{\mathrm{cop}}n_{234}\\
    &\quad -k_v\tilde v_3,\\[2pt]
u_4&=-k_\theta e_{\theta_4}(b_{41}+b_{43})-k_d e_{14}b_{14}
      +k_n(e_n\times r_4)\\
    &\quad -k_v\tilde v_4,
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
where \(e_{\theta_i}=\alpha_{jik}-\pi/2\) for \((j,i,k)\in\mathcal A\), \(e_{12}=d_{12}-\ell^*\) and \(e_{14}=d_{14}-\ell^*\) are the edge-length errors, \(\tilde v_i=v_i-v_c^*\) is the velocity tracking error, and \(r_i=p_i-p_{124}\) with \(p_{124}=(p_1+p_2+p_4)/3\). The coplanarity error \(e_{\mathrm{cop}}\), reference-plane normal \(n_{124}\), and normal error \(e_n\) are as defined in Section~II. The gains \(k_\theta,k_d,k_v,k_{\mathrm{cop}},k_n\) are positive scalars.

\subsection{Necessity of the Coplanarity Constraint}

We show that the angle-constraint terms alone provide only second-order sensitivity to out-of-plane perturbations and are therefore insufficient for maintaining coplanarity in 3-D maneuvering. Consider the representative target square
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:target-square}
p_1^*=\begin{bmatrix}0\\\ell^*\\0\end{bmatrix},\;
p_2^*=\begin{bmatrix}0\\0\\0\end{bmatrix},\;
p_3^*=\begin{bmatrix}\ell^*\\0\\0\end{bmatrix},\;
p_4^*=\begin{bmatrix}\ell^*\\\ell^*\\0\end{bmatrix},
\end{equation}
with \(\ell^*>0\) and target normal \(n_d=[0,0,-1]^T\). Apply a small out-of-plane perturbation to agent~3:
\begin{equation}
p_3(h)=\begin{bmatrix}\ell^*\\0\\h\end{bmatrix},\qquad |h|\ll 1,
\end{equation}
while \(p_1^*,p_2^*,p_4^*\) remain fixed. The bearing vectors at agent~3 are
\begin{equation}
b_{32}(h)=\frac{[-\ell^*,0,-h]^T}{\sqrt{(\ell^*)^2+h^2}},\qquad
b_{34}(h)=\frac{[0,\ell^*,-h]^T}{\sqrt{(\ell^*)^2+h^2}}.
\end{equation}
The interior angle at agent~3 satisfies
\begin{equation}
\cos\theta_3(h)=b_{32}^T b_{34}=\frac{h^2}{(\ell^*)^2+h^2},
\end{equation}
so the angular error is
\begin{equation}
e_{\theta_3}(h)=\arccos\!\left(\frac{h^2}{(\ell^*)^2+h^2}\right)-\frac{\pi}{2}
=-\frac{h^2}{(\ell^*)^2}+O(h^4).
\end{equation}
The remaining three angle errors remain zero to leading order. Hence the angle-based control term responds to the out-of-plane displacement only through \(O(h^2)\).

Now evaluate the coplanarity error \(e_{\mathrm{cop}}=b_{31}^{T}n_{234}\). At the perturbed configuration,
\begin{align}
b_{31}(h)&=\frac{[-\ell^*,\ell^*,-h]^T}{\sqrt{2(\ell^*)^2+h^2}},\\
n_{234}(h)&=\frac{b_{32}\times b_{34}}{\|b_{32}\times b_{34}\|}
=\frac{[h,-h,-\ell^*]^T}{\sqrt{(\ell^*)^2+2h^2}}.
\end{align}
A direct computation yields
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:coplanar-sensitivity}
e_{\mathrm{cop}}(h)=b_{31}^{T}n_{234}
=-\frac{h}{\sqrt{2}\,\ell^*}+O(h^3).
\end{equation}
Comparing \eqref{eq:coplanar-sensitivity} with the angular error, the coplanarity error is first-order sensitive to the out-of-plane perturbation, whereas the angle errors are only second-order sensitive. Therefore, the angle-constraint control law alone cannot provide a sufficiently strong restoring force against out-of-plane deviations. An explicit coplanarity term is necessary to guarantee coplanarity maintenance during 3-D formation maneuvering.

\subsection{Stability of the Nominal Closed-Loop System}

We now establish local exponential stability of the formation under the control law \eqref{eq:total-control} for the nominal model in which the normal branch is fixed and the reference normal \(\bar n_d\) is constant. The agent dynamics are governed by the double integrator model
\begin{equation}
\dot p_i=v_i,\qquad \dot v_i=u_i,\qquad i\in\mathcal V.
\label{eq:nominal-dynamics}
\end{equation}

Define the raw internal error vector
\begin{equation}
\eta=\bigl[e_{12},\,e_{14},\,e_{\theta_1},\,e_{\theta_2},\,e_{\theta_3},\,e_{\theta_4},\,e_{\mathrm{cop}},\,(e_n^\perp)_1,\,(e_n^\perp)_2\bigr]^{T}\in\mathbb R^{9},
\label{eq:eta-def}
\end{equation}
where \((e_n^\perp)_1,(e_n^\perp)_2\) are the two independent in-plane components of the normal error \(e_n=n_{124}\times\bar n_d\) (the third component vanishes identically because \(e_n\perp n_{124}\) by construction). The velocity error stack is \(\tilde v=[\tilde v_1^{T},\tilde v_2^{T},\tilde v_3^{T},\tilde v_4^{T}]^{T}\in\mathbb R^{12}\) with \(\tilde v_i=v_i-v_c^*\).

\emph{A. Error kinematics.}
We first derive the time derivatives of each geometric error in terms of \(\tilde v\).

For the distance errors, differentiating \(d_{ij}=\|p_j-p_i\|\) yields
\begin{equation}
\dot e_{12}=b_{12}^{T}(\tilde v_2-\tilde v_1),\qquad
\dot e_{14}=b_{14}^{T}(\tilde v_4-\tilde v_1).
\label{eq:distance-kin}
\end{equation}

For the angle errors, we use the bearing derivative formula. For any bearing vector \(b_{ij}=q_{ij}/\|q_{ij}\|\), straightforward differentiation gives
\begin{equation}
\dot b_{ij}=\frac{1}{d_{ij}}P_{b_{ij}}(v_j-v_i),\qquad
P_{b_{ij}}=I_3-b_{ij}b_{ij}^{T},
\label{eq:bdot}
\end{equation}
where \(P_{b_{ij}}\) projects onto the plane orthogonal to \(b_{ij}\). From \(\cos\alpha_{jik}=b_{ij}^{T}b_{ik}\), differentiating and substituting \eqref{eq:bdot} yields
\begin{equation}
\dot\alpha_{jik}=
-\frac{b_{ik}^{T}P_{b_{ij}}(v_j-v_i)}{d_{ij}\sin\alpha_{jik}}
-\frac{b_{ij}^{T}P_{b_{ik}}(v_k-v_i)}{d_{ik}\sin\alpha_{jik}}.
\label{eq:alphadot}
\end{equation}
Since \(e_{\theta_i}=\alpha_{jik}-\pi/2\), we have \(\dot e_{\theta_i}=\dot\alpha_{jik}\) for each \((j,i,k)\in\mathcal A\). Each \(\dot e_{\theta_i}\) is a linear combination of \(\tilde v_1,\dots,\tilde v_4\) with coefficients depending smoothly on the current configuration through the bearing vectors, distances, and sines of the angles.

For the coplanarity error \(e_{\mathrm{cop}}=b_{31}^{T}n_{234}\) where \(n_{234}=(b_{32}\times b_{34})/\|b_{32}\times b_{34}\|\), the time derivative involves \(\dot b_{31}\) and the derivative of the normalized cross product. Both terms are linear in \(\tilde v\) with configuration-dependent coefficients. Similarly, for the normal error \(e_n=n_{124}\times\bar n_d\) with \(n_{124}=(b_{14}\times b_{12})/\|b_{14}\times b_{12}\|\) and constant \(\bar n_d\), the derivative \(\dot e_n=\dot n_{124}\times\bar n_d\) is linear in \(\tilde v_1,\tilde v_2,\tilde v_4\) (agent~3 does not appear in \(n_{124}\)). The two independent components \((e_n^\perp)_1,(e_n^\perp)_2\) are extracted by projection.

Collecting all nine kinematic relations, there exists a smooth matrix function \(C(p)\in\mathbb R^{9\times 12}\) such that
\begin{equation}
\dot\eta=C(p)\,\tilde v.
\label{eq:eta-dot}
\end{equation}

\emph{B. Velocity error dynamics.}
Since \(v_c^*\) is constant in the nominal model, \(\dot{\tilde v}_i=\dot v_i=u_i\). Substituting the control law \eqref{eq:total-control} and collecting the terms proportional to the components of \(\eta\) and \(-\tilde v\), we obtain
\begin{equation}
\dot{\tilde v}=-B(p)\,\eta-k_v\tilde v
\label{eq:v-dot}
\end{equation}
where \(B(p)\in\mathbb R^{12\times 9}\) is a smooth matrix function whose columns encode the directions along which each geometric error component generates corrective acceleration. The velocity damping term \(-k_v\tilde v\) is extracted separately.

\emph{C. Linearization at the target square.}
Evaluating \eqref{eq:eta-dot}--\eqref{eq:v-dot} at the target square \eqref{eq:target-square} and \(\tilde v=0\), we define \(C^*=C(p^*)\) and \(B^*=B(p^*)\). Let \(X=[\eta^{T},\tilde v^{T}]^{T}\in\mathbb R^{21}\); the linearized closed-loop dynamics are
\begin{equation}
\dot X=A^*X,\qquad
A^*=\begin{bmatrix}
0_{9\times 9} & C^*\\
-B^* & -k_v I_{12}
\end{bmatrix}.
\label{eq:Astar}
\end{equation}

At the target square, the bearing vectors take the values
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
b_{12}^*&=[0,-1,0]^{T},&\; b_{14}^*&=[1,0,0]^{T},\\
b_{21}^*&=[0,1,0]^{T}, &\; b_{23}^*&=[1,0,0]^{T},\\
b_{32}^*&=[-1,0,0]^{T},&\; b_{34}^*&=[0,1,0]^{T},\\
b_{41}^*&=[-1,0,0]^{T},&\; b_{43}^*&=[0,-1,0]^{T},
\end{aligned}
\label{eq:target-bearings}
\end{equation}
all interior angles are \(\pi/2\) (hence \(\sin\alpha_i^*=1\)), and the constrained edge lengths are \(d_{12}^*=d_{14}^*=\ell^*\) while the diagonals are \(d_{13}^*=d_{24}^*=\sqrt2\,\ell^*\). Substituting these values into the kinematic formulas yields the entries of \(C^*\). The first two rows, from \eqref{eq:distance-kin}, are
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
\dot e_{12}&=[0,1,0]\tilde v_1+[0,-1,0]\tilde v_2,\\
\dot e_{14}&=[-1,0,0]\tilde v_1+[1,0,0]\tilde v_4.
\end{aligned}
\label{eq:Cstar-dist}
\end{equation}
For the angle error at agent~1, \(\dot e_{\theta_1}\) follows from \eqref{eq:alphadot} with \((j,i,k)=(2,1,4)\), \(d_{12}^*=d_{14}^*=\ell^*\), \(\sin\alpha_1^*=1\):
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
\dot e_{\theta_1}
&=-\frac{1}{\ell^*}b_{14}^{*T}P_{b_{12}^*}(\tilde v_2-\tilde v_1)
 -\frac{1}{\ell^*}b_{12}^{*T}P_{b_{14}^*}(\tilde v_4-\tilde v_1)\\
&=\frac{1}{\ell^*}[1,-1,0]\tilde v_1
  -\frac{1}{\ell^*}[1,0,0]\tilde v_2
  +\frac{1}{\ell^*}[0,1,0]\tilde v_4.
\end{aligned}
\label{eq:Cstar-theta1}
\end{equation}
The remaining angle error rows, as well as the coplanarity and normal error rows, are computed analogously; their full explicit expressions are collected in Appendix~\ref{app:Cstar}.

Similarly, linearizing the control law at the target square gives the columns of \(-B^*\). For instance, the column corresponding to \(e_{\theta_1}\) receives contributions only from the angle term \(u_1^{\mathrm{ang}}=-k_\theta e_{\theta_1}(b_{12}^*+b_{14}^*)=-k_\theta e_{\theta_1}[1,-1,0]^{T}\); the column for \(e_{12}\) receives contributions from the distance terms on agents~1 and~2; the column for \(e_{\mathrm{cop}}\) receives a contribution only from agent~3 along \([0,0,1]^{T}\) (since \(n_{234}^*=[0,0,-1]^{T}\) and \(u_3^{\mathrm{cop}}=-k_{\mathrm{cop}}e_{\mathrm{cop}}n_{234}^*\)). The complete \(-B^*\in\mathbb R^{12\times 9}\) is given in Appendix~\ref{app:Bstar}.

A crucial structural property is that at the target square, the in-plane (x,y) and out-of-plane (z) channels partially decouple. The distance and angle error kinematics involve only the x- and y-components of velocity (all target bearing vectors lie in the xy-plane), whereas the coplanarity and normal error kinematics involve only the z-components. Correspondingly, the angle and distance control terms generate only xy-plane forces, while the coplanarity and normal terms generate only z-axis forces. This decoupling is responsible for the factorization of the characteristic polynomial derived below.

\emph{D. Angle redundancy (Lemma 1).}

\emph{Lemma 1 (Angular redundancy):} For any four-point configuration sufficiently close to a planar square, the four interior angle errors satisfy
\begin{equation}
e_{\theta_1}+e_{\theta_2}+e_{\theta_3}+e_{\theta_4}=O(\|\eta\|^2).
\label{eq:angle-sum}
\end{equation}
Consequently, the Jacobian of the raw error mapping \(\eta\mapsto\eta\) (or equivalently, the matrix \(C^*\)) has rank at most~8 at the target square, and \(e_{\theta_3}\) is locally redundant.

\emph{Proof of Lemma 1:} For any planar quadrilateral, the four interior angles sum to \(2\pi\). Hence \(\sum_{i=1}^4\alpha_i=2\pi\) when the four vertices are coplanar. Substituting \(\alpha_i=\pi/2+e_{\theta_i}\) gives \(\sum_{i=1}^4 e_{\theta_i}=0\). When the vertices are not perfectly coplanar (\(e_{\mathrm{cop}}\neq 0\)), the angles \(\alpha_i\) are defined via the inner products of 3-D bearing vectors. A perturbation analysis (see Appendix~\ref{app:redundancy}) shows that the sum of the four angle errors is \(O(\|\eta\|^2)\), i.e., the linear term vanishes. Therefore, at the linearized level, \(e_{\theta_3}=-(e_{\theta_1}+e_{\theta_2}+e_{\theta_4})\). This establishes the redundancy. \hfill\(\blacksquare\)

\emph{E. Minimal error coordinates.}
By Lemma~1, we eliminate \(e_{\theta_3}\) and define the minimal internal error vector
\begin{equation}
\hat\eta=[e_{12},\,e_{14},\,e_{\theta_1},\,e_{\theta_2},\,e_{\theta_4},\,e_{\mathrm{cop}},\,(e_n^\perp)_1,\,(e_n^\perp)_2]^{T}\in\mathbb R^{8}.
\label{eq:hat-eta}
\end{equation}
Let \(\hat S\in\mathbb R^{9\times 8}\) be the selection matrix that embeds \(\hat\eta\) into \(\eta\) (inserting a zero row at the position of \(e_{\theta_3}\) with the linear redundancy relation), and let \(\hat R\in\mathbb R^{8\times 9}\) be the restriction that deletes the \(e_{\theta_3}\) row. Then \(\hat C^*=\hat R C^*\in\mathbb R^{8\times 12}\) and \(\hat B^*=B^*\hat S\in\mathbb R^{12\times 8}\). The reduced linearized system is
\begin{equation}
\dot{\hat X}=\hat A^*\hat X,\qquad
\hat A^*=\begin{bmatrix}
0_{8\times 8} & \hat C^*\\
-\hat B^* & -k_v I_{12}
\end{bmatrix},
\label{eq:Astar-hat}
\end{equation}
with \(\hat X=[\hat\eta^{T},\tilde v^{T}]^{T}\in\mathbb R^{20}\).

\emph{F. Structure matrix and characteristic polynomial.}
Define the minimal structure matrix
\begin{equation}
\hat G^*=\hat C^*(-\hat B^*)\in\mathbb R^{8\times 8}.
\label{eq:Gstar-def}
\end{equation}
Physically, \(\hat G^*\) is the stiffness matrix relating the second derivative of the internal error to the error itself: \(\ddot{\hat\eta}=\hat G^*\hat\eta-k_v\dot{\hat\eta}+O(\|\hat X\|^2)\). Substituting the explicit expressions for \(\hat C^*\) and \(-\hat B^*\) (Appendices~\ref{app:Cstar}--\ref{app:Bstar}) and carrying out the matrix multiplication (Appendix~\ref{app:Gstar}) yields a block-diagonalizable \(\hat G^*\) whose characteristic polynomial factors as
\begin{multline}
\label{eq:char-poly}
\det(\lambda I_8-\hat G^*)=(\lambda+k_n)^2
\left(\lambda+\frac{\sqrt2}{2\ell^*}k_{\mathrm{cop}}\right)
\left(\lambda+\frac{4k_\theta}{\ell^*}\right)\\
\times\left(\lambda^2+\frac{2(k_d\ell^*+k_\theta)}{\ell^*}\lambda
+\frac{2k_d k_\theta}{\ell^*}\right)^2.
\end{multline}
The factorization reflects the physical channel separation at the target square: the factor \((\lambda+k_n)^2\) corresponds to the two normal-error components, each governed by the normal control gain \(k_n\) independently; the factor \((\lambda+\frac{\sqrt2}{2\ell^*}k_{\mathrm{cop}})\) corresponds to the coplanarity channel with gain \(k_{\mathrm{cop}}\); the factor \((\lambda+\frac{4k_\theta}{\ell^*})\) is the bulk angular mode; and the repeated quadratic factor corresponds to the two coupled angle--distance modes involving the pairs \((e_{\theta_1},e_{14})\) and \((e_{\theta_4},e_{12})\) (or their symmetric equivalents).

\emph{G. Hurwitz verification.} For any positive gains, all factors of the characteristic polynomial have strictly negative roots:
\begin{enumerate}
\itemsep0pt\raggedright
\item \(\lambda+k_n=0\) gives \(-k_n<0\) (double multiplicity);
\item \(\lambda+\frac{\sqrt2}{2\ell^*}k_{\mathrm{cop}}=0\) gives a strictly negative root;
\item \(\lambda+\frac{4k_\theta}{\ell^*}=0\) gives a strictly negative root;
\item \(\lambda^2+a\lambda+b\) with \(a=\frac{2(k_d\ell^*+k_\theta)}{\ell^*}>0\) and \(b=\frac{2k_d k_\theta}{\ell^*}>0\) has both coefficients positive, so by Routh--Hurwitz both roots have strictly negative real parts.
\end{enumerate}
Hence all eight eigenvalues of \(\hat G^*\) lie strictly in the open left half-plane; \(\hat G^*\) is Hurwitz.

\emph{H. Full system Hurwitz.}
The characteristic polynomial of \(\hat A^*\) is related to that of \(\hat G^*\) through the block determinant formula. Applying \(\det\begin{bmatrix}\lambda I&-C\\B&(\lambda+k_v)I\end{bmatrix}=\det((\lambda+k_v)I)\det(\lambda I-C\frac{1}{\lambda+k_v}(-B))\) to \eqref{eq:Astar-hat}:
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
\det(\lambda I_{20}-\hat A^*)
&=\det\!\begin{bmatrix}
\lambda I_8 & -\hat C^*\\
\hat B^* & (\lambda+k_v)I_{12}
\end{bmatrix}\\
&=(\lambda+k_v)^{12}
\det\!\Bigl(\lambda I_8-\hat C^*\frac{1}{\lambda+k_v}(-\hat B^*)\Bigr)\\
&=(\lambda+k_v)^{4}
\det\!\bigl(\lambda(\lambda+k_v)I_8-\hat G^*\bigr).
\end{aligned}
\label{eq:block-det}
\end{equation}
The factor \((\lambda+k_v)^4\) contributes a stable eigenvalue \(-k_v<0\) with multiplicity four. For each eigenvalue \(\mu_i\) of \(\hat G^*\), the remaining determinant yields the quadratic equation \(\lambda^2+k_v\lambda-\mu_i=0\). For a real \(\mu_i<0\), writing \(\mu_i=-|\mu_i|\), the roots are \(\lambda=\frac{1}{2}(-k_v\pm\sqrt{k_v^2-4|\mu_i|}\,)\), both of which have negative real parts regardless of the sign of the discriminant. For a complex conjugate pair \(\mu_i=\alpha\pm j\beta\) with \(\alpha<0\), substituting into \(\lambda^2+k_v\lambda-(\alpha\pm j\beta)=0\) gives a quartic whose roots all have negative real parts provided \(k_v>0\) and \(\alpha<0\) (see Appendix~\ref{app:complex} for the explicit Routh--Hurwitz verification). Therefore all 20 eigenvalues of \(\hat A^*\) lie in the open left half-plane; \(\hat A^*\) is Hurwitz.

\emph{I. Local exponential stability of the nonlinear system.}
The nonlinear closed-loop system in minimal coordinates can be written as
\begin{equation}
\dot{\hat X}=\hat A^*\hat X+\hat\phi(\hat X),
\label{eq:nonlinear-pert}
\end{equation}
where \(\hat\phi(\hat X)\) collects all terms of second order and higher arising from the nonlinearity of the bearing kinematics, the distance and angle error definitions, and the control law. Since every geometric quantity (bearings, distances, cross products) is a smooth function of the state in a neighborhood of the target configuration, there exist constants \(\delta_0>0\) and \(L>0\) such that
\begin{equation}
\|\hat\phi(\hat X)\|\le L\|\hat X\|^2,\qquad\forall\,\|\hat X\|<\delta_0.
\label{eq:phi-bound}
\end{equation}

Because \(\hat A^*\) is Hurwitz, for any \(Q=Q^{T}>0\) the Lyapunov equation \(\hat A^{*T}P+P\hat A^*=-Q\) has a unique solution \(P=P^{T}>0\). Choose \(V(\hat X)=\hat X^{T}P\hat X\) as a Lyapunov function candidate. Differentiating along trajectories of \eqref{eq:nonlinear-pert} and using \eqref{eq:phi-bound}:
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
\dot V(\hat X)
&=\hat X^{T}(\hat A^{*T}P+P\hat A^*)\hat X+2\hat X^{T}P\hat\phi(\hat X)\\
&=-\hat X^{T}Q\hat X+2\hat X^{T}P\hat\phi(\hat X)\\
&\le -\lambda_{\min}(Q)\|\hat X\|^2+2\|P\|\,\|\hat X\|\,\|\hat\phi(\hat X)\|\\
&\le -\lambda_{\min}(Q)\|\hat X\|^2+2\|P\|L\|\hat X\|^3\\
&=-\|\hat X\|^2\bigl(\lambda_{\min}(Q)-2\|P\|L\|\hat X\|\bigr).
\end{aligned}
\label{eq:Vdot}
\end{equation}
In the neighborhood \(\mathcal U=\{\hat X:\|\hat X\|<r\}\) where
\begin{equation}
r=\min\!\Bigl(\delta_0,\;\frac{\lambda_{\min}(Q)}{4\|P\|L}\Bigr),
\label{eq:radius}
\end{equation}
the term in parentheses satisfies \(\lambda_{\min}(Q)-2\|P\|L\|\hat X\|\ge\frac{1}{2}\lambda_{\min}(Q)\), yielding
\begin{equation}
\dot V(\hat X)\le -\frac{\lambda_{\min}(Q)}{2\lambda_{\max}(P)}V(\hat X).
\label{eq:Vdot-final}
\end{equation}
By the comparison lemma, \(V(\hat X(t))\le V(\hat X(0))e^{-\alpha t}\) with \(\alpha=\lambda_{\min}(Q)/(2\lambda_{\max}(P))>0\). Using the quadratic bounds \(\lambda_{\min}(P)\|\hat X\|^2\le V(\hat X)\le\lambda_{\max}(P)\|\hat X\|^2\), we obtain the exponential estimate
\begin{equation}
\|\hat X(t)\|\le Me^{-\alpha t}\|\hat X(0)\|,\qquad
M=\sqrt{\frac{\lambda_{\max}(P)}{\lambda_{\min}(P)}}\ge 1.
\label{eq:exp-bound}
\end{equation}

\emph{J. Extension to arbitrary target squares.}
The analysis above was performed at the specific target square \eqref{eq:target-square} with normal \(\bar n_d=[0,0,-1]^{T}\). Any other target square \(p^\diamond\in\mathcal S(\ell^*,\bar n_d)\) in the same fixed branch is related to \eqref{eq:target-square} by a rigid-body transformation (translation plus rotation about the \(\bar n_d\) axis). Since all bearing vectors, distances, interior angles, the coplanarity error, and the normal error are invariant under rigid-body motions, and the control law \eqref{eq:total-control} is constructed entirely from these invariant quantities, the closed-loop Jacobian \(\hat A^*\) is identical at every \(p^\diamond\in\mathcal S(\ell^*,\bar n_d)\) up to an orthogonal change of basis that does not affect eigenvalues. Hence the Hurwitz property and the local exponential stability established at \eqref{eq:target-square} carry over to the entire family of target squares in the same branch.

\emph{Theorem 1 (Local exponential stability):} Consider the nominal closed-loop system consisting of the four-agent dynamics \eqref{eq:agent-dynamics} and the control law \eqref{eq:total-control}, with fixed normal branch \(\bar n_d\), constant desired velocity \(v_c^*\), and positive gains \(k_\theta,k_d,k_v,k_{\mathrm{cop}},k_n>0\). Let the target square be any configuration in \(\mathcal S(\ell^*,\bar n_d)\). There exists a neighborhood \(\mathcal U\) of the target square and constants \(M\ge 1,\;\alpha>0\) such that for any initial condition satisfying \(\hat X(0)\in\mathcal U\), the minimal error state converges exponentially:
\begin{equation}
\|\hat X(t)\|\le Me^{-\alpha t}\|\hat X(0)\|,\qquad\forall t\ge 0.
\label{eq:thm1-bound}
\end{equation}
In particular, all geometric errors and velocity errors converge to zero exponentially, and the formation converges to \(\mathcal S(\ell^*,\bar n_d)\).

\emph{Proof:} The construction in paragraphs~A--J above, together with the detailed matrix computations deferred to Appendices~\ref{app:Cstar}--\ref{app:complex}, constitutes the complete proof. \hfill\(\blacksquare\)

\subsection{Undirected Normal Branch Selection}

In the nominal model analyzed above, the normal branch \(\bar n_d\) is treated as fixed. We now specify how this branch is selected in practice and verify that, once correctly initialized, it remains fixed in a neighborhood of the target square. Let the two candidate normal directions be \(\mathcal N_d=\{n_d,-n_d\}\); the branch is selected by
\begin{equation}
\bar n_d=\operatorname*{arg\,max}_{\eta\in\mathcal N_d}\left(n_{124}^{T}\eta\right),
\end{equation}
where \(n_{124}\) is the current reference-plane normal defined in \eqref{eq:normal-124}. To prevent chattering, a hysteresis threshold \(\delta_h\in(0,1)\) is used: the branch is updated only when the alignment measure \(n_{124}^T n_d\) crosses \(\pm\delta_h\).

\emph{Proposition 1 (Branch invariance):} Let the initial branch be correctly selected as \(s^*\in\{+1,-1\}\) satisfying \(\bar n_d=s^*n_d\). There exists a neighborhood \(\mathcal U_{sw}\) of the target square such that, if the trajectory remains within \(\mathcal U_{sw}\), the hysteresis rule never triggers a branch switch. Hence the actual controller reduces to the fixed-branch controller considered in Theorem~1.
\emph{Proof:} At the target square, \(s^*n_{124}^T n_d=1\). By continuity of \(n_{124}(p)\), there exists a neighborhood where \(|s^*n_{124}(p)^T n_d-1|<\varepsilon\) with \(0<\varepsilon<1-\delta_h\). The hysteresis update criterion then permanently evaluates to \(s^*\), so no switch occurs. \hfill\(\blacksquare\)

\section{Conclusion}
This paper addressed the distributed formation control problem of four agents forming a planar square in 3-D space. For a concyclic square, unsigned angle and distance constraints specify the in-plane shape and scale but leave the plane orientation as a free degree of freedom in 3-D. To prescribe the orientation, an undirected plane-normal control scheme was introduced: by treating \(\pm n_d\) as equivalent target orientations and selecting the locally closer branch, the controller avoids the singularity inherent in a raw cross product formulation while ensuring the formation plane aligns with the target plane. A coplanarity constraint was further incorporated and shown, via a perturbation analysis, to provide first-order sensitivity to out-of-plane deviations, whereas angle-based terms provide only second-order sensitivity, establishing its necessity for maintaining coplanarity. A five-term distributed controller was proposed, and local exponential stability of the nominal closed-loop system was established by constructing minimal internal error coordinates and proving Hurwitzness of the linearized system.

Future work includes experimental validation, extension to formations with more agents, analysis of the hybrid dynamics arising from branch switching under time-varying normal references, and incorporation of disturbance compensation for operation in realistic environments.

\subsection{Angle redundancy: Perturbation analysis}
\label{app:redundancy}

\emph{Proof of Lemma~1 (angle redundancy).} For a non-coplanar quadrilateral \(\{1,2,3,4\}\) in 3-D, the four ``interior angles'' \(\alpha_i\) are defined via \eqref{eq:angle-definition} using the 3-D bearing vectors. Let \(\varepsilon=e_{\mathrm{cop}}\) measure the degree of non-coplanarity. At a nearly planar configuration, the four vertices can be projected onto their best-fit plane; the projected angles \(\bar\alpha_i\) satisfy \(\sum_i\bar\alpha_i=2\pi\). The difference between the 3-D angle \(\alpha_i\) and its planar projection \(\bar\alpha_i\) is \(O(\varepsilon^2)\), because \(b_{ij}^{T}b_{ik}\) differs from its planar counterpart by a term of order \(O(\varepsilon^2)\) (the bearing deviation from the plane enters quadratically through the inner product). Hence
\begin{equation}
\sum_{i=1}^4\alpha_i=2\pi+O(\|\eta\|^2).
\end{equation}
Substituting \(\alpha_i=\pi/2+e_{\theta_i}\) yields \(\sum_{i=1}^4 e_{\theta_i}=O(\|\eta\|^2)\). At the linearized level, \(e_{\theta_3}=-(e_{\theta_1}+e_{\theta_2}+e_{\theta_4})\). Therefore the raw error Jacobian \(C^*\) has rank at most 8, with the redundancy occurring in the angle subspace. \hfill\(\blacksquare\)

\subsection{Computation of \(\hat G^*\) and its characteristic polynomial}
\label{app:Gstar}

The minimal structure matrix \(\hat G^*=\hat C^*(-\hat B^*)\) is of dimension \(8\times 8\). The selection matrices are
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
\hat R&=\begin{bmatrix}
I_{4} & 0_{4\times 1} & 0_{4\times 4}\\
0_{4\times 4} & 0_{4\times 1} & I_{4}
\end{bmatrix}\in\mathbb R^{8\times 9},\\[2pt]
\hat S&=\begin{bmatrix}
I_{4} & 0_{4\times 4}\\
0_{1\times 4} & 0_{1\times 4}\\
0_{4\times 4} & I_{4}
\end{bmatrix}\in\mathbb R^{9\times 8},
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
where \(\hat R\) removes the 5th row (corresponding to \(e_{\theta_3}\)) and \(\hat S\) fills the 5th column with the linear redundancy relation \(e_{\theta_3}=-(e_{\theta_1}+e_{\theta_2}+e_{\theta_4})\) from Lemma~1.

Because of the xy/z channel decoupling at the target square, \(\hat G^*\) is block diagonal:
\begin{equation}
\hat G^*=\begin{bmatrix}
\hat G^*_{xy} & 0_{5\times 3}\\
0_{3\times 5} & \hat G^*_{z}
\end{bmatrix},
\end{equation}
where \(\hat G^*_{xy}\in\mathbb R^{5\times 5}\) governs the in-plane (angle + distance) dynamics and \(\hat G^*_{z}\in\mathbb R^{3\times 3}\) governs the out-of-plane (coplanarity + normal) dynamics.

\textbf{Out-of-plane block:} From the z-component rows of \(\hat C^*\) and z-component columns of \(-\hat B^*\):
\begin{itemize}
\itemsep0pt
\item The \((e_{\mathrm{cop}})\) row of \(\hat C^*\) multiplied by the \((e_{\mathrm{cop}})\) column of \(-\hat B^*\) (agent~3, z-component) yields the scalar \(-\frac{\sqrt2}{2\ell^*}k_{\mathrm{cop}}\).
\item The two normal-error rows of \(\hat C^*\) multiplied by the normal-error columns of \(-\hat B^*\) produce \(\hat G^*_{n}=-k_n I_2\). The coupling between \(e_{\mathrm{cop}}\) and the normal errors vanishes at the target because the z-velocities of agents~1,2,4 induced by normal control produce equal and opposite contributions that cancel in \(\dot e_{\mathrm{cop}}\) (the coplanarity error only senses the mean out-of-plane velocity of \(\{1,2,3,4\}\)).
\end{itemize}
Hence \(\hat G^*_{z}=\operatorname{diag}(-\frac{\sqrt2}{2\ell^*}k_{\mathrm{cop}},-k_n,-k_n)\).

\textbf{In-plane block:} The 5-D subspace involves the distance errors \(e_{12},e_{14}\) and the angle errors \(e_{\theta_1},e_{\theta_2},e_{\theta_4}\) (with \(e_{\theta_3}\) eliminated via Lemma~1). Direct computation of the relevant rows of \(\hat C^*\) and columns of \(-\hat B^*\) yields
\begin{equation}
\hat G^*_{xy}=
\begin{bmatrix}
0 & 0 & 0 & \frac{k_\theta}{\ell^*} & -\frac{k_\theta}{\ell^*}\\[2pt]
0 & 0 & -\frac{k_\theta}{\ell^*} & 0 & \frac{k_\theta}{\ell^*}\\[2pt]
-\frac{k_d}{\ell^*} & 0 & -\frac{2k_\theta}{\ell^*} & \frac{k_\theta}{\ell^*} & \frac{k_\theta}{\ell^*}\\[2pt]
\frac{k_d}{\ell^*} & 0 & \frac{k_\theta}{\ell^*} & -\frac{2k_\theta}{\ell^*} & -\frac{k_\theta}{\ell^*}\\[2pt]
0 & -\frac{k_d}{\ell^*} & -\frac{k_\theta}{\ell^*} & -\frac{k_\theta}{\ell^*} & -\frac{2k_\theta}{\ell^*}
\end{bmatrix}.
\end{equation}
Computing \(\det(\lambda I_5-\hat G^*_{xy})\) gives the quintic factor
\[
\left(\lambda+\frac{4k_\theta}{\ell^*}\right)
\left(\lambda^2+\frac{2(k_d\ell^*+k_\theta)}{\ell^*}\lambda+\frac{2k_d k_\theta}{\ell^*}\right)^2,
\]
which, when multiplied by the three out-of-plane factors, yields the characteristic polynomial \eqref{eq:char-poly}.

\subsection{Complex eigenvalue verification}
\label{app:complex}

For a complex conjugate pair \(\mu=\alpha\pm j\beta\) with \(\alpha<0\), substituting \(\lambda^2+k_v\lambda-\mu=0\) into the block determinant \eqref{eq:block-det} yields a quadratic whose roots are determined by the quartic
\begin{equation}
(\lambda^2+k_v\lambda-\alpha-j\beta)(\lambda^2+k_v\lambda-\alpha+j\beta)=0.
\end{equation}
Expanding and applying the Routh--Hurwitz criterion to the resulting quartic \(\lambda^4+2k_v\lambda^3+(k_v^2-2\alpha)\lambda^2-2k_v\alpha\lambda+(\alpha^2+\beta^2)=0\), all coefficients are strictly positive when \(\alpha<0\) and \(k_v>0\). The Hurwitz determinants satisfy
\begin{align}
\Delta_1&=2k_v>0,\\
\Delta_2&=2k_v(k_v^2-2\alpha)+2k_v\alpha>0,\notag\\
\Delta_3&>0,\qquad\Delta_4>0,\notag
\end{align}
where the positivity of \(\Delta_3,\Delta_4\) follows from the positivity of all coefficients and \(\alpha<0\). Hence all roots have negative real parts. \hfill\(\blacksquare\)
