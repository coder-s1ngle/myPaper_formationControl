Multi-agent distributed formation control using local information measurements
Jianqing LI1, Liaofei CHEN2* & Chaoyong LI3
1Space Information Research Institute and Zhejiang Key Laboratory of Space Information Sensing and Transmission, Hangzhou Dianzi University, Hangzhou 310018, China
2College of Communication Engineering, Hangzhou Dianzi University, Hangzhou 310018, China 3College of Electrical Engineering, Zhejiang University, Hangzhou 310027, China
Received November 18, 2024; accepted February 8, 2025; published online May 28, 2025

Abstract This paper investigates formation control in multi-agent systems to adjust deformation and scaling for obstacle avoidance using local information. A well-known issue with angle-based formation control is the existence of size ambiguity. Introducing additional controlled variables can potentially mitigate this problem. Therefore, unlike purely angle-based formation control methods that can only adjust deformation, our approach can also control scaling by introducing distance measurements. Specifically, all agents use their local coordinate systems and sense neighboring agents to obtain angle measurements, with only one agent measuring the distance to its neighbor. In the proposed formation control scheme, angle measurements are used to control the desired shape, while distance measurements regulate the formation scaling. Different formation control strategies for triangular and polygonal configurations are discussed, utilizing angle rigidity to ensure the stability of the final shape in polygonal configurations with only one distance measurement used for formation scaling. Additionally, by relaxing the condition of angular rigidity and utilizing the concept of formation rigidity, we propose a formation determination method to construct a rigid square configuration, in which a pair of distances measured by one agent are used to control the formation's scaling. Finally, using a simplified model of a quadrotor unmanned aerial vehicle (QUAV), simulation results validate the effectiveness of the proposed formation control scheme through several examples.
Keywords angle measurements, distance measurements, formation control, rigid formation, multi-agent systems

1 Introduction
In recent years, collaborative control of multi-agent systems has attracted widespread attention, with formation control issues receiving extensive research due to their significance across multiple application domains [1,2]. Examples include unmanned aerial vehicle (UAV) search and rescue [3], collaborative transport of ground robots [4], and satellite formation flying [5].
A key focus is addressing which variables need to be sensed and actively controlled to achieve desired formations

in multi-agent systems, as mentioned in ref. [6]. Sensors used for formation control typically include the global positioning system (GPS), radar, and cameras, which can measure positions, inter-agent distances, and bearings, respectively. The existing formation control methods are categorized based on the sensors and control variables used, including position-based, displacement-based (consensus-based) [7, 8], direction-based, distance-based, and angle-based approaches. However, a disadvantage of position-based formation control is that it typically requires a global coordinate system shared among the agents [9]. In displacement-based methods, agents measure the relative positions of their neighbors in a common reference coordinate system, necessitating

that all agents have a shared sense of direction [10, 11]. This implies that the local coordinate frames of all agents need to be aligned [12]. Achieving perfect alignment of all agents' coordinate systems is challenging due to the presence of sensor measurement noise and interference. When there is a misalignment in the agents' coordinate frames, the formation shape specified by relative positions may be distorted and exhibit non-zero translational velocity [13, 14]. In ref. [15], the theory of bearing rigidity was established, but it still requires agents to know a global reference frame. In contrast, distance/angle-based formation methods that rely solely on local information from multi-agent systems have become popular, even though they increase the complexity of their design and analysis.
Over the past few decades, distance rigidity has been extensively studied both as a mathematical topic in graph theory [16] and as an engineering problem in the application of multi-agent system formations. Distance rigidity [17] is defined based on the property that distances within a multipoint framework remain invariant under translational and rotational motions. Without requiring coordinate frame alignment, the desired formation shape described by distances was achieved using local relative position measurements in ref. [18] by employing distance rigidity theory. Moreover, a theory based on bearing rigidity was proposed [15, 19]. Agents maintain their relative orientation with respect to their neighbors to ensure the stability of the formation. However, methods solely based on bearings become ambiguous due to the lack of measurement for formation size. To address this, a bearing-based approach within the leader-follower framework was introduced [20, 21], but it requires that the maneuvering strategies of two leaders be pre-planned. With the advancement of sensor technology, angle measurements can be easily obtained through omnidirectional radar or visionbased cameras, which are cheaper, more reliable, and more accessible than distance measurements [22, 23]. Since angle measurements are scalars and do not change with motion, including translation, rotation, and scaling [24, 25], the theory of angle rigidity was established in ref. [26]. By assigning appropriate angle constraints to the agents, the desired formation shape can be achieved. However, a resulting issue is that the angles do not change with the scaling of the formation. When relying solely on angle information for control, the size of the formation becomes ambiguous when the multi-agent system stabilizes. To address this issue, a control algorithm for multi-agent systems was designed in ref. [27], which determines the formation shape through angle constraints while still using relative position measurements in the global coordinate system to scale the formation. Recently, the formation control method combining angle and distance has been studied in refs. [28, 29], and a formation control scheme with directed graph distance measurements and angle constraints has been proposed. However, these

methods impose complex directed graph distance constraints and angle measurements, increasing the difficulty of formation rigidity design. In many cases, it is necessary to manipulate the formation scale, such as to avoid obstacles [30], and the variation in pairwise distances between agents during scaling complicates the control algorithm.
Motivated by the work above, we propose a formation scaling control law based on angle and distance to address formation maintenance during obstacle avoidance. This approach requires each agent to measure only the angle information of its neighbors, with only one agent responsible for measuring distance information. Angle rigidity ensures the stability of the final formation shape, while distance control manages the scaling of the formation. The contributions of this paper are summarized as follows.
(1) Compared with ref. [26], this addresses the issue of ambiguity in controlling the formation size using only angle information. In ref. [26], when the formation system is stable, all angle constraints are satisfied. However, because angles do not change with scaling motions, the overall size of the formation is ambiguous and cannot be controlled arbitrarily. Additionally, to relax an angular rigidity constraint in ref. [26], we discuss a method using a square formation case to control the scaling by weighting a pair of distances in two different directions.
(2) Different from the rigidity formation methods that combine angle and distance-directed graph in refs. [28, 29], the scaling transformation of the formation in this paper is controlled by a single agent, eliminating the need to change a large number of desired distances between different agents and thereby simplifying the complexity of the control algorithm during the process of expansion and contraction motion.
(3) This work represents a preliminary step in addressing the problem of formation maintenance, deformation, and scaling control based on local information. Due to the difficulty in constructing a Lyapunov function, we have currently proven the local convergence of the closed-loop nonlinear formation under double integrator dynamics. In view of this, we provide sufficient conditions to ensure the stability of the double integrator formation, which offers valuable guidance for future research.
The remainder of this paper is organized as follows. Section 2 introduces the agent formation control problem. Section 3 presents the main results with different configurations. Section 4 shows the simulation results examining the performance of the proposed formation control law.

Remark 1 It should be pointed out that, for the proposed control law, the number of distance measurements is reduced. Remark 2 In view of Figure 4, note that the local bearings of each agent can be misaligned. Even if the agents lose their global sense of direction in certain complex environments, it will not affect the completion of the formation. The local bearing misalignment does not affect the interior angle i and the distance di j measurements. Regardless of the bearing orientation, the agents' formation motion is always toward the angular bisector zi j + zik of the interior angle i, and the direction of zi j + zik is constant.

5 Conclusion and discussion
This paper investigates formation control problems in multiagent systems and designs control laws that regulate deformation and formation scaling using angle and distance information. Each agent measures the angle, with only one agent measuring the distance. This approach effectively addresses the ambiguity in controlling the scale of the formation when relying solely on angle information. The overall scaling of the formation is controlled by a single agent's distance measurement. When a rigid formation scales to avoid obstacles, it does not require extensive adjustments of distance constraints between different agents. Multiple experiments have been conducted to demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed control algorithms. The results show that agent formations can navigate through narrow terrains with flexible sizes.
Currently, we can only prove the local convergence of the closed-loop nonlinear formation double integrator dynamics; global convergence remains unknown. Although the method is feasible for n agents, its performance and complexity may be affected as the number of agents increases significantly. Due to the diverse geometric shapes of formations with different numbers of agents, discussing all possible configurations in detail is impractical. For obstacle avoidance tasks, the primary goal is to avoid obstacles, and adopting a feasible formation scheme is sufficient without considering arbitrary formation shapes. Future work will address global convergence issues.
