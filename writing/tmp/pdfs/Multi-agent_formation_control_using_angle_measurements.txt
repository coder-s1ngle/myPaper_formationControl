Multi-agent formation control using angle measurements
Liangming Chen, Ming Cao, Chuanjiang Li, Xiaodong Cheng and Yuri Kapitanyuk
Abstract This paper investigates the triangular and polyg-
onal formation control problem for mobile multi-agent systems
under the constraint that each agent can only take angle
measurements. For triangular formations, due to the fact that
the sum of three interior angles always equals , the desired
triangular shape can be obtained when any two agents achieve
desired angles for which they are the corresponding vertices
of the triangle. So to achieve the desired shape of a triangular
formation, we propose to let one agent remain xed and the
other two agents move along their bisectors respectively with
respect to their two neighbors. For convex polygonal formations,
since the sum of all interior angles is constant, we are able to
use a similar control strategy to achieve the desired polygonal
shape. The stability of the closed-loop multi-agent systems is
proved using Lyapunov theory. Finally, simulation examples
illustrate the validity of the theoretic results.
I. INTRODUCTION
Formations of multi-agent systems have broad applica-
tions, e.g., Unmanned Aerial Vehicles (UA Vs) formations in
the near-Earth space [1], Autonomous Underwater Vehicles
(AUVs) formations in oceans [2], and Spacecraft Formation
Flying (SFF) in deep space [3]. Formation control has
become a hot topic in the research community of multi-
agent systems [4], [5]. The sensors used in formation control
include GPS, radar, and camera, which produce the measure-
ments of positions, inter-agent distances, and bearings [5],
[6]. Many results have concentrated on the position-based or
displacement-based formation control problem, partly due to
the convenience to apply control theories for linear systems
or gradient systems [7][10]. The disadvantage of position-
based formation control is that a global coordinate system
is usually required to be shared among the agents [6],
[11]. In distance-based formation control, relative positions
measured in each agent's local coordinate system are usually
required [12], [13]. However, in many practical applications,
it is hard for each agent to gain knowledge of the global
coordinate system or measure relative positions with respect
to neighboring agents [14], [15]. For example, in deep space,
it is difcult for satellites to get the GPS information because
there is usually no global coordinate system with respect to
the earth [16].

With the rapid development of sensor technologies, an-
gle measurements are becoming cheaper, more reliable and
accessible than relative position or inter-agent distance mea-
surements [6], [11]. Angle information can be easily obtained
by the full-range radar or vision-based camera [17]. Thus,
angle sensors are more applicable in engineering practices,
which motivates us to study the problem of multi-agent
formation control using only angle measurements. For angle-
based formation control, one agent's interior angle is usu-
ally calculated by the relative bearings with respect to
its neighboring agents. More precisely, there are mainly
three different denitions for bearing. The rst is the angle
between a sensing beam and an axis of the global coordinate
system [15], [18]. The second refers to the angle between the
sensing beam and the x-axis of the corresponding agent's
local coordinate system [19], [20]. The third is represented
in a global coordinate system by the unit vector starting from
the corresponding sensing agent along the sensing beam [21],
[22]. Correspondingly, there are research results studying the
angle-measurement based formation control problem using
each of the above denitions of bearing.
Eren et al. [18] used the second denition and established
direction-based bearing rigidity using the fact that a bearing
constraint can be transformed into a constraint on the direc-
tion that an agent is conned to move along once all agents
know the global coordinate system. In a follow-up work [14],
the triangular formation control problem was investigated,
in which some distance measurements were needed. Also
using the second denition, Bishop et al. [19], [20] studied
the triangular formation control problem using only angle
measurements; more specically in [20], Poincare-Bendixson
theorem was used to prove the asymptotic convergence of
the closed-loop formation control system. The results in
[23] extended those in [19], [20] to four-agent formations.
The distributed control of angle-constrained cyclic forma-
tions was studied in [24] using bearing-only measurements,
where local stability was proved. In [15], by using the rst
denition, stiff rigidity with mixed bearing and distance
constraints was dened and a formation control algorithm
was designed. Zhao et al. [21] established bearing rigidity
using the third denition, in which a bearing-only formation
control algorithm was constructed to guarantee almost global
convergence. In [22], translational and scaling formation ma-
neuvering control was realized via a bearing-based approach,
in which both the bearing information and relative positions
were required.
Motivated by the existing results, in this study we aim
at solving the formation control problem using only angle
measurements, in which bearing takes its meaning according

to the second denition. Two classes of formations are
considered, i.e., triangular and convex polygonal formations.
Since the sum of the three interior angles of any triangle is
, the desired triangular formation is realized when any two
agents achieve their desired angles. Thus, our control strategy
stipulates that one agent keeps static and the other two are
guided to reach their desired angles by moving along the
bisector of the corresponding interior angle. Similarly, the
sum of the interior angles of a convex polygon is constant.
Therefore, to realize the convex polygonal formation control,
one can also let one agent keep static and the other agents
move to reach their desired angles.
The main contributions of this study can be summarized
as follows. The triangular and convex polygonal formations
are realized using only angle measurements. Compared with
other proposed formation control algorithms which require
the measurements of bearings, distances or relative positions
[14], [15], only angle measurements are required in this
study, which can be realized by only installing on-board
camera. Angle-based Lyapunov functions are designed to
prove the stability of the closed-loop formation systems.
Compared with using Poincare-Bendixson theorem as is done
in [20], Lyapunov theorem offers a more general analysis
framework. Different from [21], [22], the global coordinate
system is not needed in our approach.
The rest of this paper is organized as follows. Section
II introduces basic background knowledge and the problem
formulation. In Section III, we dene the triangular formation
control problem, followed by the stability analysis of the
closed-loop system. In Section IV , the convex polygonal
formation control and the corresponding stability analysis
are given. The theoretical results are illustrated by numerical
simulations in Section V .
